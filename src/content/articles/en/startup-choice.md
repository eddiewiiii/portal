One afternoon in October 2022, I sat at my desk at Pacific Auto writing a resignation letter. I wrote slowly. Deleted, revised, deleted again. The office air conditioning hummed. A colleague at the next desk was on a phone call, voice kept low. I typed a subject line, deleted it, and in the end just wrote one sentence: “I am resigning for personal reasons,” followed by the date. The cursor hovered over the send button for about a minute. When I clicked, my palms weren't sweating. Just a tightness in my stomach.

It started two nights before. Flora had asked me to dinner at a dai pai dong in Tianhe. She said she wanted to start a parent-child study program and asked if I was in. Flora was a friend I'd known for a few years. She worked in education and had always wanted to go out on her own. On the table: a plate of stir-fried clams, two bottles of beer, and a pile of shrimp shells left by the previous customers, still uncleared. Flora spat clam shells onto the table while talking me through the numbers. How much it would cost to start from zero. What the worst case looked like. I listened, poking at the rice in my bowl with my chopsticks. The light bulb wasn't bright enough. It made her face look yellow, a bit of clam juice at the corner of her mouth.

I said things were fine for me at the big company. She said sure, things are fine, but you told me last time you wanted to build something of your own. I thought about it. I had probably said that, some night when I was working late, complaining.

The next morning at work, I sat at my desk at Pacific Auto staring at the 52% number on the dashboard. I had spent seven months pulling it up from 0.5%. When it got there I didn't feel happy. Just tired. In the afternoon there were two meetings. Someone said the next goal was to push activity to 70%. I sat in the conference room listening, and suddenly I couldn't muster any interest in dragging a number from 52 to 70. What Flora had talked about last night, starting from zero, kept turning over in my head.

After the meetings I went back to my desk, opened my email, and started writing the resignation letter. While writing, I thought about the plate of clams at the dai pai dong, the juice at the corner of Flora's mouth, the way she tapped her chopsticks on the table when she was doing the math. She had said the worst case was burning through the money and having nothing to show for it. “But at least you tried.” I remembered that clearly.

After I sent the letter, I went to find my direct manager. He was in his office, a cup of tea gone cold on the desk. He read the email, looked up at me for a moment, and asked if I'd thought it through. I said I had. He said I was doing well at Pacific Auto, the numbers were right there. I said I knew. He didn't push. “Good luck, then.”

I walked out of his office and stood in the hallway. The hallway lights were white, a little harsh on the walls. I pulled out my phone and sent Flora a message: “I quit.” She replied instantly with one word: “Do it.” Then she sent a location pin, an office space she'd been looking at, in Panyu, Guangzhou. I tapped the pin. On the map it was a grey building with nothing around it.
