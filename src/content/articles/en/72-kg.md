It was right around the new year. I stepped on the scale and saw the number 72.

Nobody called me fat. I just couldn't stand it myself. A lot of things were piling up inside with nowhere to put the energy. So I went to the gym. Had to do something.

From 72 to 63, I don't remember how much I sweated, but I remember the taste of every diet meal. Boiled chicken breast shredded into strips, a pinch of black pepper. By the third day I was questioning my life choices. Broccoli blanched and left to cool, tasting of astringent grass. Every night I'd lie awake hungry, listening to the neighbor's TV through the wall. I'd hear the host say "coming up next," then roll over and hear him flip to another channel.

The first time I noticed the faint lines on my stomach, I was standing in front of the mirror on a Tuesday morning. Fresh out of the shower, the bathroom light a bit blinding. I turned sideways and looked again. It stung a little. I wasn't moved, exactly. I just felt the months of hunger hadn't been for nothing.

I started logging my weight every day, every bite I ate, whether I'd drunk enough water. My phone notes were full of numbers. Those numbers made me feel like life was still in my hands. The other stuff I couldn't control, but how much I ate this meal, that was up to me.

Working out became a habit. When I'd shake holding a plank, I'd tell myself, one more second. When I couldn't get up from a squat, I'd think, this weight is nothing. Easy to say. My legs were genuinely shaking.

The thing I said most these six months: I wasn't like this before.

What was I like before? If someone didn't reply to my message, I'd script an eighty-episode drama in my head. If I liked someone, I'd rather die than say it first, thinking being proactive meant being cheap. When I was upset, I'd act magnanimous, swallow it all until I hurt inside, and the other person would think I genuinely didn't care.

Some things happened. Someone taught me what obsession was, and someone taught me what it felt like to actually like someone. I won't write the specifics. The short version: I cut my heart open, took a look, stitched it back together, and kept using it.

I still take the initiative when I like someone. But the mindset is different. I'm proactive because I chose you, because I want to honor my own feelings. I'm not begging you to love me. I'm not afraid of getting hurt, because I know even if I do, I can fix myself. Last month I cooked myself a bowl of noodles one night, cracked an egg in, ate it, broke a sweat, and felt like everything could pass.

Life is the same day repeated.

Up at seven. Line 2 to Line 6. At the office by 8:45. Work until 7:45. Ride back to the subway. Home by nine to prep food, set tomorrow's lunch in the rice cooker. In bed by eleven. Asleep by twelve.

This dense schedule might look dull to others. To me it's order, it's an anchor. It tells me that no matter what happened yesterday, the seven o'clock alarm will ring. I'll have a place to go, things waiting to be done.

The work is busy and the commute is long, but I'm grateful. This isn't a coasting job. It needs my brain and my investment, and it gives me a sense of achievement. Last month I negotiated a new salary. The number made me feel I was doing okay. My palms sweated talking to the boss. Afterward I walked out of the meeting room, went to the restroom, splashed water on my face, and smiled at myself in the mirror.

On weekends if I don't go out, I cook. I buy the best ingredients, spend an hour or two prepping, make something that tastes as good as a restaurant. Last week I made braised pork ribs. I had the heat too high when reducing the sauce, almost burned it. I killed the flame, stood there with the spatula for three seconds, then laughed. It turned out fine. A friend collapsed on my sofa and ate two bowls of rice, said "you should open a restaurant." I said no. I can't do service, I'd fight with the customers.

Watching my friend sink into my sofa in a food coma was more healing than anything. For the length of one meal, I pull myself out of the spinning gears and turn back into a living person.

There's one more thing from these six months worth recording. Near the end of spring, I gathered the courage to tell someone "I don't want to miss this." Now it's summer, and we've already drifted apart.

The relationship was short. So short that looking back now feels like watching a slightly hazy film. But it did change me. I used to think being proactive meant being cheap, that whoever said it first lost. With him, I broke that rule. I cooked for him, wrote these long menus where every dish name hid something I wanted to say. These were things I thought I'd never do. Too unlike me. But I did them.

I wasn't trying to please him. I just wanted to honor my own feelings. I gave it everything, so I have no regrets.

He taught me other things too. Like setting boundaries. I could change for him, but I couldn't lose myself. I told him I needed my own space, told him I didn't like the way he talked about money. I used to be afraid of conflict, only wanted surface peace. But for the relationship to continue, I forced myself to face the problems. Even though we still broke up, I'd held my line.

I understand now that loving someone and holding on to yourself can exist at the same time.

The day we broke up I deleted all his contact info. Afterward I sat on the edge of the bed, staring at the empty chat list, spacing out for five minutes. Then I got up, poured myself a glass of water, and opened the rice cooker to check if tomorrow's lunch was done.

It was done.

That's been my life these six months. Ordinary, but solid. I'm not the person who used to agonize in the April sunset anymore. I stand on my own ground, still wobble occasionally, but I know I won't fall again.

As I write this, the wind picks up outside. Someone downstairs is walking their dog. The dog is running fast, dragging the owner along. The owner is cursing.

Tomorrow the seven o'clock alarm will ring. Line 2 to Line 6. At the office by 8:45. Lunch is already in the rice cooker.

That's enough.
