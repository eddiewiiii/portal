Xiao Tang stood behind the bar, left hand shaking the tin, right hand wiping a glass. A July evening in Changsha, only two tables seated. The air conditioning was on, the warm yellow light above the bar falling on the back of his hand, the tendons jumping with each shake. I sat at the innermost seat, a glass of AiWan Breeze in front of me, vodka as the base, grapefruit and cranberry juice meeting in the glass, the rim faint pink.

This was Xiao Tang's fourth month at Puzi. He came after A-Jie left. The day A-Jie left for Shenzhen, I handed him the drink list for the City Inspiration Series, ten drinks, ten lines, each followed by a story of Changsha. He copied the recipes and asked, brother, what does the mist of Yuelu Mountain taste like. I said, climb it once and you will know. I had done brand work for ten years, and in that moment I found I could not answer either.

He went at six the next morning. A May morning on Yuelu Mountain has thin mist, the stone steps wet. That night he stood behind the bar testing. Vodka as base, creme de cassis poured in, the color turning grayish purple. He squeezed in pineapple juice, tasted, shook his head. The ninth version he finally nodded, pushed the glass toward me. I took a sip, that damp, grass-not-yet-cleared taste, it really was like that mountain in the early morning. That drink is called Yuelu Mountain.

Each of the ten signatures has its own origin. Peach Secret uses rum and milk, white peach syrup hidden inside, sweet first then astringent. Half-Drunk is brandy plus rum, lemon juice holding it down. Espresso Dream was A-Jie's last, vodka with coffee liqueur and espresso. Miss Tiffany is rum with coconut milk, blue curacao turning it pale blue. Blue Shanghai also uses blue curacao. Lao Zhou asked why a Changsha bar would name a drink after Shanghai. I said the Xiang River is blue too. Summer Lychee has no alcohol, just guava and lychee juice. Starfruit Garden piles tropical fruit together. Purple Haze is grape with rose syrup and Sprite, with rose on the palate after.

Xiao Tang spent a full week on AiWan Breeze. I told him to go sit at Aiwan Pavilion for an evening. He actually went. He came back and said, the wind is cool, but it does not feel cold on the skin. He remixed it, vodka as base, grapefruit, cranberry, peach syrup, the ratio changed five times. The last version he brought me, pale pink, the peach sweetness first, then a faint bitter tail of grapefruit. I said, this is it. He smiled, the first time he smiled in the shop.

Half fireworks, half poetry. That is the line I gave Puzi. The fireworks is the wok behind the bar. Siamese sour-spicy crispy chicken sizzles when it comes out. The moment mountain pepper beef jerky hits the table, mujiangzi oil fills the whole room. The poetry is hidden in the drinks. Peach Secret is the crush never spoken. AiWan Breeze is the wind that does not feel cold. I also built the concepts of Emotional Asylum and introvert-friendly bar, with names on the menu like Solo Happiness Combo and Don't Want to Talk Today.

It makes me sheepish to say it. These concepts are easier to talk about when the place is full. Puzi sits above Sam's Club in the Poly mall. The foot traffic downstairs is real, but those people are there to buy things. They push their carts out and leave. They do not come upstairs for a drink. On weeknights the bar often has two or three tables. Sometimes I wonder whether an Emotional Asylum still counts if nobody comes in.

Last month a guy came in alone, sat at the innermost seat, ordered Peach Secret, finished it, and ordered another. I asked if he was thinking of someone. He did not answer. When he paid he said, in college he liked a girl, never told her. I said this drink was mixed for that kind of person. He smiled and said he would come back. His second visit was three days later. He ordered Espresso Dream. The last sip was slow. I asked how his day was. He said, okay. Just one word.

Another guest came four days in a row, the same AiWan Breeze every time. On the fourth day he asked who named the drinks. I said, me. He said, nice. None of these people wandered in off the street. With all that foot traffic at Sam's downstairs, not one person came up for a drink because they happened to be passing by. They were drawn by something I cannot quite name. Maybe the line on the menu. Maybe the drink. The crowd is small, but each person who comes stays with the drink for a long time.

When Xiao Tang collected the glass I checked the time, eleven forty. The lights were still on. I sat there without moving, thinking about how the mist of Yuelu Mountain ended up in a glass, and the guy who drank Espresso Dream so slowly. Downstairs, Sam's had closed. The parking lot was empty. The warm light above the bar was still on, falling on Xiao Tang's hand as he wiped the glasses.
