When I joined Penguin Explore in late 2022, the study-tour brand had already been running for a while. But the visual system was a mess: the logo was a hand-drawn penguin holding a magnifying glass, the color palette had half a dozen variants, and the typography changed with every version.

If we couldn't even explain our own visual style to ourselves, no wonder users couldn't either. I took one look at that logo and thought: this needs a redo.

But the first thing I did wasn't to brief a designer. I went back to twenty parents and asked them all the same question: Why did you choose us?

Three phrases kept coming back: "reliable service," "attentive mentors," "my kid likes it." That was the direction for the visual upgrade, not pretty for its own sake, but making parents feel those three things at a glance.

The design review took two hours. The meeting room got loud.

The designer brought three options. The first was a cartoon penguin, round, cute. Some of the team liked it; others thought it was too childish. The second was minimalist linework: sophisticated, sure, but it had nothing to do with "study tours." The third option, the designer wasn't sure about it himself, so he put it last.

Up close, the left contour of that penguin was a human face.

The room went quiet for a few seconds. Someone said, "That's interesting." Someone else said, "Is it too complicated?" I stared at that option for a long time. 

Then I pointed at it and said, "This one."

The reasoning was actually simple. A study-tour brand needs to feel exploratory and approachable at the same time. A cartoon penguin would be childish; a minimalist one would be cold. But a symbol with a story, a penguin from far away, a human face up close, that carries two layers. The penguin stands for expertise and warmth. The human face stands for people at the center. The eye as the focal point: seeing, exploring.

For parents: deep blue and white. Blue for professionalism and safety, white for possibility, black as the accent for visual tension. For kids: we hid little easter eggs throughout the visual materials, penguin footprints, map elements, magnifying glass details, hinting at "a world you can explore."

The whole process took three weeks. New visual system, all materials updated, new brand video shot.

Sometime after it launched, I was scrolling through my feed and saw a photo from an event. It was a background wall, shot through a crowd, compressed by a phone screen, the logo was already a bit blurry. But someone in the comments asked: is this a Penguin Explore event?

I screenshotted it and sent it to the designer. He replied with an emoji.

A clear symbol doesn't need a slogan to explain it. Changing a logo isn't hard. What's hard is making sure something recognizable lives inside that symbol.
