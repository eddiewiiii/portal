2019年六月，广州。我坐在出租屋的书桌前，面前摊着笔记本电脑，屏幕上是简历。经历一栏排满了旅游：平安银行在那儿，华夏银行在那儿，香港航空也在，还有南澳大利亚旅游局一场推介会。这些都是我在租租车做商务拓展时对接的客户。第二天要去面试易车，一个汽车互联网公司，市场岗位。简历上跟汽车有关的字一个都找不到。

我把“南澳大利亚旅游局推介会”那一行选中，删掉，又撤销，删掉，又撤销。光标在那一行闪。窗外有人在楼下炒菜，油烟味飘上来。删掉这些旅游客户，简历就空了大半；留着，对方一看就知道我是做旅游出身的。那天晚上改到十二点多，最后留了几行，把“旅游”两个字尽量往不显眼的地方塞。

第二天去面试。面试官三十多岁，戴黑框眼镜，桌上一杯没怎么动过的咖啡。他翻了几下简历，抬头问我：“你做过汽车吗？”我说没有。他追了一句：“那你觉得你能做汽车互联网的市场吗？”我当时手心有点出汗，但嘴上没停。我说我在租租车对接过租车公司，租车和买车的人在决策路径上有一部分重合，我做过的事情有一部分能迁移过来。他没接话，在简历上写了几个字。我不知道他写了什么。

后来我拿到了offer。入职之后才知道，他跟别人说我“答得挺实诚，没瞎编”。

2022年，我在太平洋汽车待了七个月之后出来创业，做企鹅探学。这一次没人面试我了。我自己问自己：做过亲子研学吗？没有。搭过从零开始的东西吗？也没有。那天晚上在客厅坐到很晚，茶几上摊着商业计划书草稿，旁边一杯凉了的茶。问来问去，能给自己交代的只有一件事：我会找渠道。把一个陌生领域讲清楚，这事练过。被拒绝了厚着脸皮继续聊，也练过。这些跟亲子研学没什么关系，跟旅游和汽车也搭不上。但它们在哪儿好像都用得上。

回长沙之后，有一次跟一个做餐饮的朋友聊天，他想转行。问我跨行业难不难。我说行业知识可以补，真正卡人的是你坐到面试官对面的时候，怎么让对方信你那些跟这个行业无关的经验。他说你说的我听不懂。我笑了，说我也说不清楚，就是当时面试的时候手心出汗，嘴没停。

那天晚上从他店里出来，街上人不多，路灯把影子拉得很长。我走到路口等红灯，手插在口袋里，摸到一张揉皱的收银小票。

June 2019, Guangzhou. I sat at the desk in my rented apartment, laptop open, my resume on the screen. The experience section was packed with tourism. Ping An Bank was there. Huaxia Bank was there. Hong Kong Airlines too, plus a promotional event for the South Australia Tourism Board. All clients I had handled doing business development at Zuzuche. The next day I was going to interview at Yiche, an automotive internet company, for a marketing role. Nowhere on that resume could you find a single word related to cars.

I highlighted the line “South Australia Tourism Board promotional event,” deleted it, hit undo, deleted it, hit undo. The cursor blinked on that line. Outside, someone downstairs was cooking. The smell of hot oil drifted up through the window. Delete those tourism clients and the resume would be mostly empty. Keep them and the interviewer would see immediately that I came from tourism. I edited until past midnight. In the end I kept a few lines, tucking the word “tourism” into the least conspicuous spots I could find.

The next day, the interview. The interviewer was in his thirties, black-framed glasses, a cup of coffee on his desk that he barely touched. He flipped through the resume a few times, looked up, and asked: “Have you ever worked in cars?” I said no. He followed up: “Then do you think you can do marketing for an automotive internet company?” My palms were a little damp, but my mouth kept going. I said I'd worked with rental car companies at Zuzuche, that people who rent cars and people who buy cars overlap in part of their decision-making path, that some of what I'd done could transfer over. He didn't respond. He wrote a few words on my resume. I had no idea what.

I got the offer. After I joined, I found out he'd told someone I “answered honestly, didn't make things up.”

In 2022, after seven months at Pacific Auto, I left to start Penguin Exploration. This time nobody interviewed me. I asked myself: have you ever done parent-child study programs? No. Have you ever built something from zero? Also no. I sat in the living room late into the night, a draft of a business plan spread on the coffee table, a cup of tea gone cold beside it. After all the asking, there was only one thing I could tell myself for certain: I knew how to find channels. Explaining an unfamiliar field clearly, I had practiced that. Getting rejected and keeping the conversation going with a thick skin, practiced that too. None of it had anything to do with parent-child study programs. Nothing to do with tourism or cars, either. But it all seemed usable wherever I went.

After moving back to Changsha, I was talking with a friend who ran a restaurant. He was leaning on the counter with a dishcloth over his shoulder, the kitchen closed, his last waiter already gone home. He wanted to switch industries. He asked me if crossing over is hard. I said industry knowledge you can pick up. What really gets you is sitting across from an interviewer, trying to make them believe your experience, all of it from outside their industry, is worth something. He said he didn't understand what I meant. I laughed. I said I couldn't really explain it either. Just that during that interview my palms sweated, and my mouth didn't stop.

That night I walked out of his shop. Not many people on the street. The streetlight stretched my shadow long. I reached the intersection and waited for the red light, hands in my pockets, and felt a crumpled receipt in one of them.
