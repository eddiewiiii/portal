凌晨两点十七分。我记得很清楚，因为放下手机之前我瞄了一眼屏幕右上角的时间。

那天是周五。我躺床上刷朋友圈，刷到一条很日常的动态。真没什么特别的，就是那个人发的日常碎片的Plog。九宫格，配了一首歌。但我发现自己看了很久，久到不太对劲。手指停在屏幕上，没往下滑。

我到底在看什么？想从里面翻出点什么来？

后来我把手机扣在床头柜上，盯着天花板。空调嗡嗡地响。脑子里冒出来一个念头，冒出来的时候我自己都吓了一跳：我的存在，有没有意义？

挺可笑的。我知道。凌晨两点，为一个九宫格Plog失眠。搁以前我会骂自己矫情，翻个身也就睡了。但那天晚上没翻过去。

因为那不是第一次了。

我以前觉得，嫉妒就是性格有问题。是那种该藏着掖着、不太好意思说出口的东西。后来读了《身份的焦虑》，才慢慢意识到，嫉妒其实是个信号。我以前嫉妒别人，不是对方真的有多好，是我心里自动把对方当成了裁判。他有的我没有，就说明我不够好。

凌晨两点刷朋友圈，跟嫉妒是一回事。只不过嫉妒的时候你盯着别人拥有的东西，深夜刷朋友圈的时候你盯着别人发出的信号。都是在等一个回应，一个能证明你存在的回应。

第二天醒来，这念头还没走。

我坐在床边发了一会儿呆。窗外的光已经白了。手机震了一下，是外卖到了。我点的是昨晚失眠时候下单的筒骨粉，送达时间是早上七点半。外卖小哥在楼下按了三遍门铃我才下去取。

嗦粉的时候我一直在想，为什么一个回得很慢的消息，会让人觉得像在等一个判决？为什么一段安静，会让我开始怀疑自己是不是不够重要？连那个曾经和我有过最深交集的人，我还是会掉进同样的坑里。在沉默和没说出口的话中间，反复琢磨，反复消耗自己。

那阵子我正好在读《身份的焦虑》。这本书最早是有人问我最近在看什么，我随手拍了封面发过去。没想到几个月后，我会在满脑子乱糟糟的想法里把它读完。书就扔在床头柜上，跟手机、眼镜、半杯隔夜的水摆在一起。那些关于焦虑的文字一下子涌过来，背后全是我们对话之后，那些没说清楚的空白。

书里有一段话是这么写的：

> 有一种焦虑，焦虑到能毁掉我们一大段人生：我们害怕自己够不到这个社会定下的成功标准，害怕因此被剥去尊严，不再被尊重。

看了两遍，又看了第三遍。

粉汤凉了。我把剩下的一点喝完，有点涩。

那段时间我想明白了一件事。我深夜刷朋友圈，跟别人没关系。是我自己的事。是那种没被选择的恐惧。是我的存在，好像不足以让某个人为我停留。哪怕那个人的故事已经翻篇了，哪怕新的章节正在开始，这种感觉还是会赖着不走。

德波顿管这叫"身份的焦虑"。那种想被看见、被尊重、被爱的渴望。不是被所有人，是被那些你在意的人。

我有一次跟我妈视频，她问我最近怎么样。我说挺好的。她说你气色不太好，是不是没睡好。我说嗯，最近有点忙。她看了我一眼，没接话。然后说，家里做的腊肉寄给你了，记得收。

挂掉视频我才意识到，我跟我妈也是这样。她说"气色不太好"的时候，我在等她说"别太累了"。她没说，我就开始琢磨，是不是我表现得还不够疲惫，所以她没当回事。你看，连我妈，我都在等一个回应来证明自己被在意。

后来慢慢想明白，成功是个会自己往前跑的目标。社会就这么设计的，让你一直跑，一直追，一直消费。你刚够到一个，下一个马上冒出来。没完没了。

我们这代人有意思。被反复告知你生而平等，却比谁都容易觉得自己不够好。因为我们在拿自己的幕后，去比别人的精修预告片。拿自己没滤镜的日常，去比旧爱回忆里加了柔光的温暖，去比新关系里模棱两可的信号。

但这本书真正留给我的，是一个词，叫做视角。

那些爱你的人，爱的不是你精心排版的主页。他们爱的东西，你根本不会写进个人简介里。是你半夜乱七八糟的念头，是不需要费力解释的沉默，是那个"本来就够了"的你。

上周末我去了一趟超市。结账的时候排在我前面的是一个老头，买了两袋米、一捆葱、一盒鸡蛋。他掏出手机扫码，扫了三次没扫上。收银员小姑娘很有耐心，帮他调出付款码，又帮他把手机举到扫码器前面。老头嘴里一直念叨"谢谢你啊小姑娘，这个我不太会"。小姑娘笑了一下说没事爷爷，下次您来我还帮您。

我站在后面看了一会儿。那个老头走出超市的时候，步伐很慢，但很稳。

我拎着自己那袋东西往回走，太阳正好。路边有个小孩在骑平衡车，摔了一跤，爬起来接着骑。

我突然想，我一直在跑一场比赛。一场必须被看见、被选择、被记住的比赛。但我好像根本没报名。那条终点线，我以为只要某个人关注我，就证明我有价值，每次我快靠近，它就又往前挪了。

也许重点从来不是赢。

那天晚上我没有刷朋友圈。我九点多就困了，关灯就睡。半夜醒过一次，摸到手机看了一眼时间，三点零四。没有打开任何一个app。又睡了。

第二天早上是被楼下收垃圾的车吵醒的。六点四十。窗帘缝里漏进来一道光，正好照在床头柜上那本《身份的焦虑》上。书还翻在那一页，折了一个角。

我起来给自己倒了杯水，站在窗边喝。楼下那个老头又出来买菜了，手里拎着跟昨天一样的布袋子。

我想，也许下一次半夜翻来覆去睡不着的时候，我还是会胡思乱想。但至少我知道，天总会亮的。楼下会有人去买菜，收垃圾的车会来，楼下杨裕兴粉店六点半就开门。

这就够了。

2:17 a.m. I remember clearly, because I glanced at the top-right corner of the screen before I put the phone down.

It was a Friday. I was in bed scrolling through my feed and landed on a very ordinary post. Nothing special, just someone's daily Plog. Nine-grid, with a song attached. But I caught myself staring at it for a long time. Too long. My finger rested on the screen, not swiping down.

What was I looking at? What was I trying to dig out of it?

I put the phone face-down on the nightstand and stared at the ceiling. The air conditioner hummed. A thought surfaced, and when it did, I startled myself: does my existence mean anything?

Ridiculous. I know. Losing sleep over a nine-grid Plog at 2 a.m. In the past I would've called myself melodramatic and rolled over and slept. But that night I didn't roll over.

Because it wasn't the first time.

I used to think jealousy was a character flaw. The kind of thing you should hide, not really admit to. Then I read Status Anxiety and slowly realized jealousy is actually a signal. When I was jealous of someone, it wasn't that they were really that great. It was that I'd automatically cast them as a judge. Whatever they had that I didn't meant I wasn't good enough.

Scrolling through feeds at 2 a.m. is the same thing as jealousy. When you're jealous, you stare at what someone else has. When you're scrolling late at night, you stare at the signals someone else sends out. Both are waiting for a response, one that proves you "count."

The next morning, the thought was still there.

I sat on the edge of the bed and zoned out for a while. The light outside had already turned white. My phone buzzed. The delivery had arrived. I'd ordered a bowl of noodle soup during my insomnia the night before, scheduled for 7:30 a.m. The delivery guy rang the doorbell three times before I went down to get it.

While eating the youtiao I kept thinking: why does a slow reply feel like waiting for a verdict? Why does silence make me start wondering if I'm not important enough? Even with the person I'd had the deepest connection with, I'd fall into the same trap. Turning over the silences and the unsaid things, wearing myself down.

Around that time I happened to be reading Status Anxiety. Someone had asked what I was reading, and I'd snapped a photo of the cover and sent it over. I didn't expect that months later, I'd finish the book with a head full of tangled thoughts. The book sat on the nightstand, next to my phone, my glasses, a half-glass of overnight water. The words on anxiety hit me all at once, and behind them were all the blank spaces left after our conversations, the things never quite said.

There's a passage in the book:

> A worry, so pernicious as to be capable of ruining extended stretches of our lives, that we are in danger of failing to conform to the ideals of success laid down by our society and that we may as a result be stripped of dignity and respect.

I read it twice. Then a third time.

The soup had gone cold. I finished the remaining half. Cold soup has a slightly chalky taste.

During that period I figured something out. My late-night scrolling had nothing to do with anyone else. It was my own thing. The fear of not being chosen. The sense that my existence wasn't enough to make someone stay. Even after that person's chapter had closed, even as a new one was starting, that feeling wouldn't leave.

Alain de Botton calls it "status anxiety." The desire to be seen, to be respected, to be loved. Not by everyone. By the people who matter to you.

Once I was on a video call with my mom. She asked how I'd been. I said fine. She said I didn't look great, asked if I'd been sleeping enough. I said yeah, just been busy lately. She looked at me for a moment, didn't say anything. Then she said, "I sent you the cured meat we made. Remember to pick it up."

After I hung up, I realized I do the same thing with my mom. When she said "you don't look great," I was waiting for her to say "don't push yourself too hard." She didn't, so I started turning it over: was I not showing enough exhaustion, so she didn't take it seriously? You see, even with my mom, I was waiting for a response to prove I was cared about.

Later, I slowly came to understand: success is a target that moves on its own. That's how society is designed, keep you running, chasing, consuming. You reach one, and the next one appears immediately. Endless.

Our generation is funny. Repeatedly told "you were born equal," yet more likely than anyone to feel we're not good enough. Because we compare our behind-the-scenes to everyone else's polished trailer. Our unfiltered daily life against the soft-focus warmth of an ex's memories, against the ambiguous signals of a new relationship.

But what the book really left me with is one word: perspective.

The people who love you don't love your carefully formatted profile page. They love things you'd never put in a bio. The messy thoughts you have at midnight. The silence that doesn't need explaining. The version of you that was "already enough."

Last weekend I went to the supermarket. At checkout, the guy in front of me was old, buying two bags of rice, a bundle of scallions, a carton of eggs. He took out his phone to scan the code, tried three times, didn't work. The cashier girl was patient, helped him pull up the payment code, held his phone up to the scanner for him. The old man kept muttering "thank you, young lady, I'm not very good at this." She smiled and said "no problem, Grandpa, I'll help you next time you come too."

I stood behind them and watched for a while. When the old man walked out of the supermarket, his steps were slow, but steady.

Walking home with my own bag, the sun was just right. A kid on the sidewalk was riding a hoverboard, fell, got back up, kept riding.

I suddenly thought: I've been running a race. A race where I had to be seen, chosen, remembered. But I never actually signed up. That finish line I thought proved I had value if someone paid attention to me, every time I got close, it moved further away.

Maybe the point was never to win.

That night I didn't scroll my feed. I got sleepy around nine, turned off the light, slept. Woke up once in the middle of the night, reached for my phone to check the time, 3:04. Didn't open a single app. Went back to sleep.

The next morning I was woken by the garbage truck downstairs. 6:40. A sliver of light came through the curtain gap, landing right on Status Anxiety on the nightstand. The book was still open to that page, with a corner folded.

I got up, poured myself a glass of water, stood by the window drinking it. The old man from yesterday was out buying groceries again, carrying the same cloth bag.

I thought, maybe next time I'm lying awake in the middle of the night, I'll still spiral into all those thoughts. But at least I know: the sky always gets light. Someone will go buy groceries downstairs, the garbage truck will come, the noodle shop opens at 6:30.

That's enough.
