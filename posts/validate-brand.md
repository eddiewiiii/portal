在广州南沙的一个研学基地，是我第一次组织企鹅探学的研学活动。

那天有二十多个孩子，跑来跑去，家长站在旁边刷手机。活动结束了，有个妈妈问我：你们下次还有吗？我说有。她说哦，然后走了。没加微信，没留电话。

我站在那个活动中心的门口，看着家长带着孩子一个一个离开。场地空了，灯还亮着。那场活动做得不差，孩子玩得开心，导师也认真。但它跟"企鹅探学"这四个字没什么关系。

当时的团队已经在做品牌和研学产品的设计了，但产品之间没有联系。用户参加完一场就走，留不下印象。我面临的问题很具体：怎么让活动本身变成品牌的一部分。

后来我们做的第一件事，是把活动从"一场一场做"变成"一个产品来设计"。每场活动不是独立的，而是属于某个主题系列，自然探索、城市探访、科技创新，每个系列有固定的视觉风格、命名规则、叙事逻辑。孩子第一次参加觉得好玩，第二次认出风格，第三次就能自发向别人推荐。

第五场活动的时候，我们开始在现场设了一面"品牌墙"。用视觉化的方式展示企鹅探学的理念和体系，家长等待的时候自然会看到。活动结束后，每个家庭会收到一份"探索报告"，孩子的照片、这次活动带回家的成果、下一场活动的预告。

导师在活动过程中也会有意识地传递品牌理念，但不是背书，是通过故事和互动自然流露。有个导师叫小林，有次带自然探索系列，孩子们在捡树叶。一个孩子问他，为什么要捡树叶？小林说，因为每片树叶都不一样，就像你们每个人。后来这个孩子的妈妈在朋友圈发了这段对话，配了活动现场的照片和品牌话题。

单场转化率后来做到了六成。不是因为销售话术厉害，是因为转化发生在体验里，不是发生在推销里。用户在离开之前，已经对品牌产生了信任。

我们还设计了一个老带新的轻量化机制。分享活动照片到朋友圈配品牌话题，或者邀请一位好友参加下一场活动，双方都能获得一份孩子喜欢的伴手礼。每场活动结束后，我们会花15分钟做复盘，记录哪些设计让用户愿意分享、哪些环节没到位。

第二十场活动结束的那天晚上，我坐在活动中心外面的台阶上整理物料。有个之前参加过的孩子拉着妈妈的手走过来，指着我身上的企鹅logo说："妈妈，就是那个企鹅。"

他妈妈笑了笑，加了我微信。

The first time I hosted a Penguin Explore study-tour session, it was at a community activity center in Nansha, Guangzhou.

Twenty-something kids running around, parents standing by scrolling their phones. When it was over, one mom asked me: will you have another one? I said yes. She said "oh," and left. Didn't add my WeChat, didn't leave a number.

I stood at the entrance of the activity center and watched parents leaving with their kids one by one. The room emptied. The lights were still on. The session hadn't been bad, the kids had fun, the mentors were serious. But it had nothing to do with the words "Penguin Explore."

The team was already working on the brand and study-tour products, but the products weren't connected to each other. Users came to one session and left without a trace. The problem in front of me was concrete: how do you make the activity itself part of the brand?

The first thing we did was stop treating each session as a one-off and start treating it as a product. Each session belonged to a themed series, Nature Exploration, City Walks, Tech Innovation, and each series had its own visual style, naming convention, and narrative arc. A kid who came once and had fun would recognize the style the second time, and recommend it to a friend the third.

By the fifth session, we'd set up a "brand wall" on-site, a visual display of Penguin Explore's philosophy and system, something parents naturally looked at while waiting. After each session, every family received an "exploration report", photos of their kid, what they brought home, a teaser for the next session.

Mentors also passed on the brand philosophy during the session, not as a script, but through stories and interaction. There was a mentor named Xiao Lin. During a Nature Exploration session, the kids were picking up leaves. One kid asked him: why are we picking up leaves? Xiao Lin said: because every leaf is different, just like every one of you. Later, that kid's mom posted the exchange on her feed, with photos from the session and the brand hashtag.

Single-session conversion eventually reached sixty percent. Not because the sales pitch was good, because conversion happened inside the experience, not after it. By the time users left, they'd already built trust with the brand.

We also designed a lightweight referral mechanism: share session photos to your feed with the brand hashtag, or invite a friend to the next session, both sides get a small gift the kid would like. After each session, we'd spend fifteen minutes debriefing: which designs made people want to share, which moments didn't land.

The night the twentieth session ended, I was sitting on the steps outside the activity center, sorting materials. A kid who'd been to a previous session came over holding his mom's hand, pointed at the penguin logo on my shirt, and said: "Mom, that's the penguin."

His mom smiled and added my WeChat.
